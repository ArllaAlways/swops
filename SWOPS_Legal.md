## Approved Terminology

These distinctions are not cosmetic — they have legal weight. Use approved terms across all site copy, contracts, terms, and communications.

| Avoid | Use instead | Why |
|---|---|---|
| home swap / home swapping | reciprocal guest stay | "swap" implies exchange of possession, closer to sublease framing |
| swap dates | stay dates | avoids transactional language |
| sublease / sublet | guest stay / licence to occupy | sublet implies tenancy; licence does not |
| tenant while away | host | reframes the relationship |
| accommodation | hospitality / a place to stay | "accommodation" implies commercial provision |

**The one exception:** SWOPS uses "swop" and "swopper" as brand terms — these are fine. The legal risk is specifically in calling the *arrangement* a "swap" or "home swap" in formal/legal contexts. In contracts and terms, always use "reciprocal guest stay."

**Plain-English description:** A SWOPS stay is **mutual housesitting**. Each Swopper goes on holiday and has the other stay at their place while they're away — exactly what a housesitter does, except both parties do it for each other simultaneously. This framing is accurate, familiar, and carries no legal baggage. Use it freely in onboarding, FAQs, and marketing copy. Avoid it in formal legal documents where "reciprocal guest stay" is the correct term.

## Legal Framing — Reciprocal Guest Stays

### The Core Argument

SWOPS is a matching service that facilitates **mutual hospitality between renters** — not a subletting product, not commercial accommodation.

The legal distinction that matters:

| | Subletting | Reciprocal Guest Stay |
|---|---|---|
| Exclusive possession granted? | Yes | No |
| Commercial transaction? | Typically yes | No |
| Requires landlord consent? | Yes (VIC & NSW) | Generally no |
| Head-tenant/sub-tenant hierarchy? | Yes | No — both parties are peers |
| Lease breach risk? | High | Low–moderate |

### What Makes SWOPS Defensible

Both parties are renters. Both are simultaneously guests in each other's homes. Neither profits. There is no head-tenant extracting value from a sub-tenant. Subletting law is designed to prevent commercial exploitation of leased premises — SWOPS doesn't do that.

Supporting factors that courts would weigh in SWOPS's favour:
- Reciprocal nature (both parties are simultaneously guests)
- No payment between users
- Short fixed durations
- Tenant retains set of keys and right to return
- No public listing — private matching only

### State Legislation

**VIC (Residential Tenancies Act 1997)**
Subletting requires landlord consent and grants the sub-renter exclusive possession. Short-term guest stays are generally permissible without consent if non-commercial and brief (commonly 7–14 days depending on lease terms).

**NSW (Residential Tenancies Act 2010)**
Same framework applies — consent required for sublets, guests generally fine without it.

### The Grey Area

The absence of a tenant during a stay could imply exclusive possession has been ceded, even without a formal subletting arrangement. The no-cash element helps but isn't conclusive on its own. Short durations and retained possession rights are the critical supporting factors.

## Exclusive Possession — The Key Test

### Definition

The legal right to sole use and control of premises, to the exclusion of all others — including the landlord and the tenant themselves.

### Key Precedents

- **Radaich v Smith [1959] HCA 45** — foundational Australian case establishing the substance-over-form test
- **Street v Mountford [1985] UKHL 4** — exclusive possession as the hallmark of a tenancy
- **Swan v Uecker [2016] VSC 313** — Victorian Supreme Court ruling that Airbnb short stays constituted sub-leases

### Applied to SWOPS

Courts look at substance, not labels. The argument rests on these practical facts being genuine and exercisable:

- Both parties are **simultaneously** absent from and present in a home — neither has exclusive use
- Each party **retains a set of keys** and the right to re-enter their own home at any time
- Either party can **revoke access** at any time
- No tenant/sub-tenant hierarchy exists — both parties occupy the same structural position

---

## Why Swan v Uecker Doesn't Apply

*Swan v Uecker* (and most Airbnb-related rulings) turned on two factors: **payment for accommodation** and a **one-way power dynamic** where one party profited by granting the other exclusive use.

SWOPS inverts both. No payment flows between users. Both parties are peers. Neither profits. The reciprocal structure means neither party can be said to have granted the other exclusive possession — because each has simultaneously accepted the same terms as a guest.

### The Slippery Slope Argument — Addressed

*If renters can swap homes, doesn't that logically support their right to sublet for cash while away?*

No — and the law already draws this line:

- Non-commercial mutual exchange sits within the same territory as having a housesitter or long-term guest — broadly accepted
- Cash subletting creates a financial relationship, a separate tenancy, and a profit motive — the things tenancy law specifically regulates
- Equity extracted through a reciprocal stay (both parties save on travel) is fundamentally different from cash flow (one party profits at the property's expense)
- No reported case law connects non-cash home exchanges to broader sublet rights claims

## HomeExchange as Comparable Precedent

HomeExchange (200,000+ members globally, operating in Australia) explicitly advises renters they can participate, framing swaps as non-subletting due to the absence of payment. They recommend checking individual leases, but there are **no reported legal cases against renters using the platform in Australia**.

SWOPS's renter-only, intra-city model is arguably more defensible — less tourism-driven, more community-based.

> No swap-specific case law exists in Australia as of early 2026. This is genuinely novel territory — not inherently doomed, and potentially worth establishing.

## Agreement Clauses That Support Licence Framing

These clauses must reflect genuine, exercisable rights — not paper protections. Courts apply substance over form.

1. **Tenant retains keys** — physical demonstration of retained possession
2. **Right to return** — tenant can re-enter the property at any time
3. **No exclusive possession granted** — explicit denial in writing
4. **Revocability** — either party can cancel the arrangement at any time
5. **Short fixed durations** — maximum stay length defined (e.g., 5 nights)
6. **No payment between parties** — explicitly stated; platform fee only
7. **Simultaneous stays** — where possible, framing that both parties are guests at the same time reinforces the reciprocal (non-hierarchical) nature

### On Revocability

A revocability clause signals a **licence** (permission that can be withdrawn) rather than a lease (a fixed interest in land). For this to hold up, it must be genuine — build real cancellation tools into the platform and log revocations if they occur.

### What is a swop? (onboarding / FAQ / marketing)

> It's mutual housesitting. You go on holiday — someone stays at your place. They go on holiday — you stay at theirs. That's it.

### Platform disclaimer (site / FAQ)

> SWOPS is a matching service, not a tenancy product. Before you register for a stay, check your lease for any clauses about short-term guests or third-party access. If you're unsure, ask your property manager — framing it as "a friend staying while I'm away" is often the easiest approach. SWOPS doesn't provide legal advice and can't review your lease for you.

### Short version (banner / inline use)

> Check your lease before you swop. Most short-term guest stays are fine — but your lease is your responsibility, not ours.

### Registration / terms acceptance copy

> By registering, you confirm you have reviewed your tenancy agreement and satisfied yourself that participating is consistent with your obligations as a tenant. SWOPS facilitates introductions between renters; it does not create, modify, or take responsibility for any tenancy arrangement.

### Damage / trust copy (FAQ or onboarding)

> Look, if someone breaks your favourite mug, work it out with them. If they do real damage, you have their full name, their address, and their ID from the video call. You've got everything you need to take it to small claims if they're a total nightmare. But remember: they're currently sleeping in your bed while you're in theirs. That's the best insurance policy on earth.

### What if something breaks? (FAQ)

> Then you talk to each other. SWOPS is a matchmaker, not an insurance broker. You're swapping with another renter who has as much to lose as you do. If you need a 50-page PDS and a guarantee, stick to a hotel. If you're cool with a handshake and a bit of mutual trust, you're in the right place.

### Cleaning expectations (house rules / onboarding)

> Leave it like you found it. Strip the beds, empty the fridge, and don't leave your hair in the drain.

## Practical Advice for Swoppers

This section can be adapted for onboarding, FAQs, or help content.

- **Check your lease.** Look for sections on "occupants," "guests," or "visitors." If it specifies limits (e.g., no more than 7 days), stick to them.
- **If in doubt, ask.** Contact Tenants Victoria (VIC) or Tenants' Union of NSW for free advice, or review your lease with them. Landlords can't unreasonably restrict social life.
- **Keep it short.** Stays of 2–4 nights sit firmly within guest territory.
- **Retain your keys.** Never hand over sole access — keep a set yourself.
- **Risks:** You're responsible for any damage or nuisance caused by guests. In rare cases, if a landlord discovers and misinterprets it as subletting, they might issue a notice — but you'd have strong grounds to challenge at VCAT/NCAT.

## Key Risks

| Risk | Notes |
|---|---|
| Exclusive possession finding | If courts treat stays as sublets, users face potential lease termination |
| Damage liability | Tenants remain liable for damage caused by guests |
| Misleading claims | Overstating legal safety could expose SWOPS to liability |
| Inconsistent lease terms | Some leases prohibit any third-party access — no universal rule applies |

### Platform responsibilities

- Always advise users to check their individual lease and seek landlord consent where uncertain
- Include clear disclaimers — "SWOPS provides general information only. We are not lawyers. Check your lease."
- Monitor ongoing tenancy law reforms — no-fault eviction bans (2025–2026) signal a tenant-friendly shift that may normalise flexibility
